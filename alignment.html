Founder Alignment: V1 Product Decision

Understanding our options and making a decision we can all execute on

The Core Question

We agree on the problem: families have unpredictable free time at tournaments, making it impossible to book activities. We agree on the solution concept: cluster games to create predictable free time.

The question is: How do we handle field/court constraints?

Two Mental Models

Chris's Model NEW TOURNAMENT TYPE

Core idea: Build the tournament around field capacity. The tournament IS the product.

  • 4 fields = 16-team tournament. Period.
  • ALL teams get clustered scheduling
  • The entire tournament is premium-priced
  • TDs make same/more money (higher price per team)
  • No VIP tier—everyone gets the RoadGame experience

Paradigm shift: "We're not fitting into existing tournaments. We're creating a new tournament type that families will pay more for."

Karl's Model VIP TIER

Core idea: Retrofit RoadGame into existing tournament structures with limited capacity.

  • TD wants 24 teams but only has 4 fields
  • RoadGame format only fits 16 teams
  • 16 teams get "VIP" clustered scheduling
  • 8 teams get traditional scattered scheduling
  • VIP sells out because everyone wants it

Reality check: "Most tournaments have scheduling constraints. We need to work within existing structures, not replace them."

The Wave Concept: A Middle Ground?

There's a third option that might satisfy both views: Waves.

How Waves Work

Instead of one tournament cycle per day, run two complete cycles (waves) by extending the operational day:

Model Fields Teams Day Length TD Revenue Family Free Time
No Waves 4 16 8am - 3pm (7 hrs) $8,000 6+ hours (everyone same time)
Two Waves 4 32 8am - 10pm (14 hrs) $16,000 7 hours (morning OR afternoon)
Pool play with rest = 6 slots × 70 min = 7 hours per field
14-hour day ÷ 7 hours = 2 complete waves
2 waves = 2× the teams = 2× the revenue

Wave Schedule Example (4 fields, 32 teams)

Wave Teams Play Time Free Time
Morning Wave 16 teams (Pools A-D) 8:00 AM - 3:00 PM 3:00 PM - 10:00 PM (7 hours)
Afternoon Wave 16 teams (Pools E-H) 3:00 PM - 10:00 PM 8:00 AM - 3:00 PM (7 hours)

Key insight: Waves don't compress games or remove rest. They extend the operational day to fit two complete tournament cycles. Every team still gets clustered games with rest AND 7 hours of guaranteed free time.

What "VIP" Means with Waves

If we use waves, VIP scheduling becomes meaningful:

  • VIP = Wave Selection — Teams pay extra to choose Morning or Afternoon wave
  • Non-VIP — Teams are assigned a wave by the tournament
  • Everyone gets clustered scheduling — No one gets scattered games

Note: This is different from Karl's original VIP concept where some teams get scattered schedules. With waves, everyone gets the RoadGame experience—VIP just lets you choose WHEN.

Karl raised a valid point: "Access to field space is the #1 barrier for TDs."

V1 Product Decision

For V1, we need to pick ONE model to test. Here are the options:

RECOMMENDED FOR V1

Option A: Single Wave (Chris's Model)

What: One tournament, all teams get RoadGame format, no VIP tier.

Why V1: Simplest to build and test. Proves the core value proposition: do families actually book activities when they have predictable free time?

Test: 16-team tournament, 4 fields, everyone done by 3pm.

Option B: Two Waves (Hybrid)

What: Morning + Afternoon waves, VIP = wave selection.

Why V2: More complex scheduling. Requires proving V1 works first. Adds revenue upside but also operational complexity.

Test after V1: 32-team tournament, 4 fields, two waves.

Option C: VIP Tier (Karl's Model)

What: Some teams get clustered, some get scattered.

Concern: Creates two-tier experience in same tournament. Harder to test—which tier caused the results?

Recommendation: Avoid for V1. Muddies the data.

Proposed V1 Scope

Model: Option A (Single Wave, all teams get RoadGame format)

Tournament size: 16 teams, 4 fields, 2 days

Format: One pool per field, teams alternate play/rest

Schedule: 8am - 3pm pool play, everyone free 3pm onwards

What we're testing:

  • Do families notice and value the predictable free time?
  • Do families actually book activities during free windows?
  • Does the TD see value in the format?
  • Can we connect families with local vendors?

V2 opportunity: If V1 proves families will book activities, then test waves to increase TD revenue.

Critical: Sport-Specific Constraints

The wave model doesn't work equally for all sports. Game length determines wave viability:

Sport Game Length Slot Duration Waves/Day RoadGame Fit
Basketball 32-48 min 50-60 min 3-4 Excellent
Soccer 40-80 min 60-90 min 2-3 Excellent
Flag Football 40 min 50-55 min 3 Excellent
Lacrosse 32-48 min 60-70 min 2-3 Excellent
Volleyball 35-75 min 60-90 min 2 Good (variable)
Ice Hockey 36-51 min 60-75 min 2-3 Good (facility limits)
Baseball/Softball 60-180 min 90-180 min 1 only Not viable

Key insight: Baseball and softball games are too long and unpredictable for the wave model. The core RoadGame thesis (clustered games + waves) only works for timed sports with games under 90 minutes.

V1 Recommendation: Start with Soccer—optimal combination of market size, wave viability, and manageable weather risk. Basketball is highest-viability but soccer has a larger market.

What We All Agree On

  • The problem is real: Scattered free time prevents families from booking activities
  • The solution concept works: Clustered scheduling creates predictable free time
  • There's revenue opportunity: Both for TDs (premium pricing) and RoadGame (booking commissions)
  • Field constraints are real: We need solutions that work within facility limits
  • We need to test: The only way to know if this works is to run a real tournament

Next Steps

  2. Find a pilot TD — Someone willing to run a RoadGame-format tournament
  3. Build minimum tooling — Schedule generator, family communication, vendor directory
  4. Run the pilot — Measure family engagement and booking behavior
  5. Decide on V2 — Based on pilot results, add waves/VIP if warranted
