Step 4

Possible Paths

Given what we've learned, here are four directions we could take. Each assumes a different problem is the "real" one. We need to align on which path—if any—we want to pursue.

A

The Discovery Problem

Core assumption: Families have free time but don't know what to do with it. They don't know what's nearby, what can handle groups, or how to book for 20 people.

What we'd build

A curated marketplace for tournament families. "Here's what's near your tournament, what's good for groups, and how to book it."

How we'd make money

  • Commission on bookings (10-15%)
  • Featured listings from vendors
  • Premium features for team organizers
How to validate: Talk to team moms. Do they struggle to find group-friendly venues? Have they tried to organize team dinners? What was hard about it?
B

The Coordination Problem

Core assumption: Team moms struggle to organize group activities. Herding 12 families to dinner is chaos. The problem isn't finding things—it's coordinating people.

What we'd build

Team coordination tools: group voting on activities, RSVP tracking, payment splitting, communication. "Teamsnap for off-field activities."

How we'd make money

  • Subscription for team organizers
  • Transaction fees on group bookings
  • Upsell to clubs/leagues
How to validate: Shadow a team mom during a tournament. Watch how she organizes team activities. What tools does she use? What's frustrating?
C

The TD Revenue Problem

Core assumption: TDs want new revenue streams but don't have access to local vendor relationships or the tools to monetize family experience.

What we'd build

A platform that connects TDs with local sponsors and vendors. We do the sales work, bring the deals, TDs get a cut. "Sponsorship-as-a-service for tournaments."

How we'd make money

  • Percentage of sponsorship deals we facilitate
  • Platform fee for vendor matching
  • Premium TD tools for larger operators
How to validate: Ask our TD founder: Would you pay for someone to bring you sponsor deals? Have you tried to get local sponsors? What happened?
D

No Real Problem

Core assumption: Families are fine with how tournaments work. The "pain" is manufactured or too small to support a business. Wave scheduling exists and people aren't demanding it.

What we'd do

Walk away. Redirect our energy to a different opportunity. This is a valid outcome if the evidence points here.

Signs this is true

  • TD founder says families don't complain about schedules
  • Team moms say organizing activities is easy enough
  • Wave scheduling exists but hasn't taken over—suggesting limited demand
  • No one is willing to pay more for "better" tournament experience
How to validate: If our conversations reveal that the problems are smaller than we thought, or that existing solutions are "good enough," we should be honest about it.

These paths aren't mutually exclusive—we might find that A and B are both true, for example. But we need to start somewhere.

Update: Based on our research, we're proposing a specific direction that combines elements of Path A and B. See the proposal for details.
