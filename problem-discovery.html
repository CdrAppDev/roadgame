Problem Discovery

Before we build anything, we need to understand the problem. This page helps us align on what we're actually solving.

The Uncomfortable Truth

We may not know what problem we're solving.

Our research revealed that wave scheduling already exists (some tournaments have offered morning/afternoon sessions for 30+ years). Scheduling software already handles game clustering. So what's actually the opportunity?

What We Thought vs. What We Found

  • Thought: Wave scheduling is a novel innovation. Found: It already exists.
  • Thought: TDs don't know how to cluster games. Found: Software like Fastbreak AI already does this.
  • Thought: Parents complain about scattered schedules. Found: Complaints are more about burnout, travel costs, too many tournaments.

This doesn't mean there's no opportunity. It means we need to go back to basics and actually understand what problem exists, for whom, and why it hasn't been solved.

Interview Guide: TD Founder

Have a conversation with your TD founder. Don't pitch—just listen. Ask these questions and write down exactly what he says.

Understanding the Current State

"Walk me through how you schedule a typical tournament. What's the process?"

Listen for: pain points, tools used, time spent, constraints

"Do you ever run tournaments with morning and afternoon sessions/waves?"

If yes: "Why? How does it work?" If no: "Why not? What's stopping you?"

"What do families complain about most?"

Listen for: is it schedules? Costs? Something else entirely?

"What's the hardest part of your job as a TD?"

Don't lead. Let him tell you.

"If you could wave a magic wand and fix one thing about running tournaments, what would it be?"

Testing Our Assumptions

"Do teams ever ask you for clustered game times? Like 'can we play all our games in the morning?'"

If yes: "How often? What do you tell them?"

"Do you think families would pay more for a tournament where they're guaranteed to be done by 2pm?"

"What would make you want to change how you schedule tournaments?"

Listen for: what would actually motivate a change?

Interview Guide: Families

Talk to 5 families who have attended travel tournaments in the past year. Not friends—strangers if possible. At the field, on Facebook groups, wherever.

Understanding Their Experience

"Walk me through your last tournament weekend. What did you do?"

Let them tell the story. Don't prompt.

"What was frustrating about it?"

"What did you do between games?"

"Did you try to do anything fun with the family—restaurants, activities, sightseeing?"

If yes: "How did that go?" If no: "Why not?"

"If you could change one thing about tournament weekends, what would it be?"

Testing Willingness to Pay

"Would you pay an extra $50-100 per tournament if you were guaranteed to be done by 2pm every day?"

"If someone showed you a list of restaurants and activities near the tournament that could accommodate your team, would you use it?"

Possible Problems to Explore

Based on our research, here are some problems that might actually exist. Use the interviews to validate or invalidate these.

Hypothesis A: The problem is discovery, not scheduling

Families have free time but don't know what to do with it. They don't know what's nearby, what can handle groups, or how to book for 20 people. The opportunity is a curated marketplace for tournament families.

Hypothesis B: The problem is coordination, not free time

Team moms struggle to organize group activities. Herding 12 families to dinner is chaos. The opportunity is team coordination tools—group booking, voting, communication.

Hypothesis C: The problem is TD revenue, not family experience

TDs want new revenue streams but don't have access to local vendor relationships. The opportunity is sponsorship/vendor facilitation for TDs—we bring the vendor deals, they get a cut.

Hypothesis D: There is no problem

Families are fine with how tournaments work. The "pain" is manufactured. If interviews reveal this, we should walk away or pivot entirely.
