Step 5

Decision Point

We've laid out what we believed, what we found, what we don't know, and the possible paths forward. Now we need to make some decisions as a founding team.

What We Need to Align On

1. Do we agree on the problem?

Each founder should be able to articulate the problem we're solving in one sentence. If we have three different answers, that's a red flag we need to address.

2. Which path do we want to pursue?

Path A (Discovery), Path B (Coordination), Path C (TD Revenue), or Path D (Walk Away). We can test multiple hypotheses, but we need a starting point.

3. What's our first validation step?

Before building anything, what conversations or experiments do we need to run? Who talks to whom? What are we trying to learn?

4. What would make us stop?

What evidence would convince us this isn't worth pursuing? Being clear about our "kill criteria" helps us avoid sunk cost thinking.

Conversation Starters

For the TD founder

"You've run tournaments for years. Why don't you use wave scheduling—morning session, afternoon session? What's the real blocker?"
"What do families actually complain about? Not what we assume—what do they literally say to you?"
"If I could bring you local sponsors and vendors who'd pay to reach your families, would that be valuable? Why or why not?"

For all founders

"In one sentence, what problem are we solving? Write it down before discussing."
"If we learned tomorrow that wave scheduling is easy and TDs just don't care, what would we do? Is there still a business?"
"What do we know that others don't? What's our actual unfair advantage here?"

Possible Outcomes

Proceed with conviction

We align on the problem, pick a path, and define a concrete first step to validate. We have a clear hypothesis and know what would prove us wrong.

Pivot the focus

We realize the original thesis was wrong, but we see a different opportunity in this space. We reframe and restart the validation process.

Pause or walk away

We can't align on the problem, or the evidence suggests the opportunity isn't real. Better to learn this now than after building something no one wants.

The goal isn't to be right about our original thesis. The goal is to find something real—a problem worth solving, for people who will pay, that we're uniquely positioned to address. If that's here, great. If not, we'll find it somewhere else.
