Step 3

Open Questions

These are the things we don't know. The gaps in our understanding that need to be filled before we can move forward with confidence.

About the Problem

What problem are we actually solving?

We assumed "scattered schedules" was the problem. But wave scheduling already exists. If TDs can do this today, what's stopping them?

What we need: Honest conversation with our TD founder about why he doesn't use wave scheduling.

Do families actually want predictable free time?

We assumed families would value this. But parent complaints focus on burnout, cost, and politics—not scattered schedules. Is this a real pain point or an assumed one?

What we need: Conversations with actual tournament families about their experience.

Why don't more TDs use wave scheduling?

Possible reasons: wave preference creates conflict (everyone wants morning), no financial incentive, complexity, or just inertia. We don't know the real answer.

What we need: Our TD founder's perspective on this specific question.

About the Solution

Would families book activities if free time was predictable?

Even with guaranteed free time, would families actually book team dinners, activities, or excursions? Or would they just rest at the hotel?

What we need: Evidence of demand. Do team moms already organize group activities? How hard is it?

Would TDs participate in revenue share?

Our model assumes TDs want a cut of activity bookings. But maybe they just want their tournament to run smoothly and don't want the complexity.

What we need: Our TD founder's honest reaction to this model.

Is the scheduling the innovation—or something else?

If wave scheduling is solvable by existing software, what's our actual value add? Maybe it's the marketplace. Maybe it's the TD relationship. Maybe it's the brand. We need to be clear.

What we need: Founder alignment on what we're actually building.

About Us

Do we agree on what problem we're solving?

Each founder may have a different view of the opportunity. If we're not aligned on the problem, we'll build the wrong solution.

What we need: Each founder to articulate the problem independently, then compare.

What's our unfair advantage?

We have a TD on the team. Is that enough? What do we know or have access to that others don't?

What we need: Honest assessment of our competitive position.

These questions don't have to stop us. But we need to be honest that we're making assumptions—and be willing to change direction if the answers surprise us.
