Step 3

Open Questions

Our research answered some questions and raised new ones. Here's what we now know and what we still need to learn.

Questions We've Answered

Why don't more TDs use wave scheduling?

We now know the key blockers—and it's not what we assumed.

Answer: Staggered schedules aren't "bad scheduling"—they're intentional. Large clubs send 15 teams with only 6 coaches. TDs stagger games (U11 at 9am, U12 at 11am, U13 at 2pm) so one coach can cover multiple teams sequentially. Wave scheduling would put all those games in the same 4-hour block, creating overlaps. TDs prioritize big clubs (their main customers) over family convenience. Additional factors: stay-to-play economics (12-hour footprints force hotel stays), parking logistics (wave handoffs cause chaos), and inertia.

Do families actually want predictable free time?

Yes—but it's more nuanced than we thought.

Answer: 59% of families try to combine tournaments with activities. They DO want this. But: (1) They want GROUP activities with the team, not individual bookings; (2) Bracket unpredictability makes advance booking nearly impossible; (3) Fatigue and cost compression reduce willingness to book; (4) The "parking anchor" keeps them stuck at the venue.

Is the "hostage experience" real?

We validated this with hour-by-hour research.

Answer: Yes. Families spend 60-70% of tournament day NOT watching games. A typical 10-12 hour day includes only 2-3 hours of actual game time. The rest is waiting, driving between fields, and "liminal gaps" of dead time.

New Questions From Research

Is "team mom" the right customer?

Research suggests families want GROUP activities. The team mom is the one coordinating these. But we haven't validated she'd pay for a tool.

What we need: Interviews with team moms. What's their biggest coordination pain? Would they pay for group booking help? How much?

Can we work with tournaments that already use wave scheduling?

Volleyball, some soccer tournaments already do this. Could we target these tournaments specifically for the activity marketplace?

What we need: List of tournaments that already use wave scheduling. Do families at these tournaments book more activities?

What kind of "last-minute flexibility" would make activity booking work?

Bracket unpredictability is a major blocker. Could we partner with venues that offer flexible cancellation? On-demand booking? Group holds?

What we need: Conversations with activity providers (escape rooms, restaurants, mini golf) about flexibility options.

Can we change the club coaching model?

The staggered schedule exists to accommodate under-staffed clubs. Wave scheduling would require clubs to hire more coaches. Is there any way to influence this—or is it a structural reality we must accept?

What we need: Understanding of club economics. Why do clubs under-staff coaches? Is it cost? Availability? Would parents pay more for better-staffed clubs?

Strategic Questions

Should we pivot from TD to team mom?

Our original thesis targeted TDs. Research suggests the team mom might be a better customer. But that's a fundamentally different business.

What we need: Founder discussion on whether we want to pursue this pivot.

What's our unfair advantage now?

We have a TD on the team. But if TDs aren't the customer, is that still valuable? What do we know that others don't?

What we need: Honest reassessment of our competitive position given new research.

Accept staggered schedules or work around them?

Staggered schedules are intentional, not broken. Three options: (1) Accept them and help families cope with the chaos, (2) Target tournaments that already use wave scheduling (volleyball, some soccer), (3) Target smaller tournaments without big-club dependencies. Which is most achievable?

What we need: Founder alignment on which strategy to pursue.

We've made progress—we know more than we did. But each answer has raised new questions. The path forward requires choosing which questions to answer next.
