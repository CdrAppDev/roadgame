Step 3

Questions Answered

Our research answered the key questions about whether a disruption opportunity exists. Here's what we now know.

Questions We've Answered

Would families pay for "tourn-acations"?

The core question: is there real demand for a better tournament experience?

Answer: YES. Families pay 200-300% premiums for predictable, vacation-integrated experiences. Cooperstown charges $10,000/team (vs $400-700 standard). Ripken charges $3,000+/week. Scheduling chaos is parents' #1 complaint, yet they've normalized it. When given the option, they pay for better.

Would clubs participate in family-first tournaments?

Even if families want this, would teams actually register?

Answer: YES. Coach shortage is NOT universal. Three segments have dedicated coaching: (1) Premium clubs (ECNL, MLS NEXT) mandate dedicated coaches, (2) Volleyball has built-in dual-coach model, (3) Small volunteer clubs have natural 1:1 ratios. These segments would PREFER wave scheduling.

Do the economics work?

Can a family-first tournament be profitable with lower field utilization?

Answer: YES. The math works with 40-60% premium registration + week-long format + destination appeal. Cooperstown sold 80% stake for $116M. Ripken is expanding to 4 locations. Surf Cup generates $120M annual economic impact. Revenue shifts from hotel rebates to premium registration.

Has anyone tried this and failed?

Is there evidence this model doesn't work?

Answer: NO failures documented. Volleyball universally adopted wave scheduling with no reversions. No documented cases of family-first tournaments failing because of the model. The barrier is economic structure preference, not proven failure.

Do families have agency in tournament selection?

If coaches/clubs choose tournaments, can families influence the choice?

Answer: NUANCED but workable. Coaches make direct decisions, BUT families choose which teams to join. Tournament access is now a recruitment differentiator. Teams market "Cooperstown access" to attract families. Families can vote with their feet at team selection time.

Can family-first be a competitive advantage?

Would "better scheduling" actually drive loyalty and growth?

Answer: YES. Dance/cheer competitions now compete on "runs on time." Studios explicitly choose competitions based on scheduling reliability. i9 Sports built $100M+ business on family-first convenience. "No stay-to-play" is a differentiator that Ripken explicitly markets.

Remaining Questions

Which destination markets are underserved?

Cooperstown has baseball. Ripken has multiple sports. Where are the gaps?

What we need: Market analysis of destination tournament coverage by sport and region. Where is there unmet demand?

Build new or partner with existing operators?

Should we create tournaments from scratch or partner with existing operators who want to adopt the model?

What we need: Conversations with tournament operators about partnership interest. Assessment of build vs. partner economics.

Which sport/age group to start with?

Volleyball already does wave scheduling. Baseball has Cooperstown. What's the best entry point?

What we need: Analysis of sport-specific dynamics, club staffing models, and destination appeal by sport.

How do we acquire the first clubs?

Even with a great tournament, how do we get teams to register for something new?

What we need: Go-to-market strategy. Relationships with premium clubs, volleyball programs, and small volunteer clubs.

The key strategic questions are now execution-focused, not validation-focused. The research confirms the opportunity exists. The question is how to capture it.
