Step 2

What We Found

We did deep research across academic sources, industry publications, Reddit forums, and tournament director perspectives. Here's what we learned.

Each finding is backed by sources. View our research sources and confidence levels →

Validated: The Problem is Real

✓ The market is huge

Youth sports travel is a $19.2 billion market (pre-COVID), projected to grow to $52+ billion. 35+ million kids participate in organized youth sports. Families spend $1,200-1,500 per tournament weekend.

✓ The "hostage" experience is real

Families spend 60-70% of tournament day NOT watching games. A typical 10-12 hour day includes only 2-3 hours of actual game time. The rest is waiting, driving between fields, and "liminal gaps" of dead time.

The "parking anchor" effect is real: once you find a spot at a crowded complex, leaving feels risky. Families stay and wait rather than risk losing their spot.

✓ Families DO want "tourn-acations"

59% of families try to combine tournaments with family activities. The demand exists. But there are major blockers to actually booking anything (see below).

Validated: Wave Scheduling Exists

Wave scheduling isn't new

The Poughkeepsie Columbus Day Tournament has offered morning/afternoon sessions since 1988. Volleyball uses wave scheduling universally. This isn't an innovation we need to invent.

✓ We now know why it's not universal

1. Coaching Conflicts: One coach managing multiple age groups can't be at morning AND afternoon games. This is the #1 blocker.

2. Stay-to-Play Economics: TDs get 10-20% of hotel revenue. Longer stays = more money. No incentive to compress schedules.

3. Parking Logistics: Wave handoffs require coordinated parking turnover most venues can't handle.

4. Inertia: "We've always done it this way" + fear of the "ripple effect" from schedule changes.

Complicated: Activity Booking

~ Families would book activities... but it's complicated

Blockers to activity booking:

  • Bracket unpredictability: "You don't know if you're playing at 10am or 4pm until midnight."
  • Fatigue: Parents are too exhausted to organize activities
  • Cost compression: Already spent $1,500; reluctant to spend more
  • Social gravity: Families want GROUP activities with the team, not individual bookings

Key Insight: The customer might be "team mom"

Research suggests families want GROUP activities with the team. The person coordinating this is typically the "team mom"—texting 20 parents trying to find a restaurant that can seat everyone. She might be the real customer.

What This Challenges

✗ Original thesis: Sell wave scheduling to TDs

TDs don't receive schedule complaints as a top issue. Their pain points are refs, weather, and parent behavior. They're motivated by competence, autonomy, and belonging—not ROI pitches. And the coaching conflict problem means wave scheduling often isn't possible regardless of software.

✗ Original thesis: Individual activity marketplace

Families don't want individual bookings. They want GROUP experiences. Bracket unpredictability makes advance booking nearly impossible. And fatigue + cost compression reduce willingness to book anything.

What This Means

"The real blocker isn't scheduling software—it's coaching conflicts. And the real customer might not be the TD—it might be the team mom."

Our research suggests a potential pivot in framing:

  • From: "Fix scattered schedules" → To: "Enable tourn-acations despite bracket chaos"
  • From: Target tournament directors → To: Target team moms / group coordinators
  • From: Individual activity booking → To: Group booking + last-minute flexibility
