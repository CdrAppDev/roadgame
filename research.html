Step 2

What We Found

We did the research. Some of it validated our thesis. Some of it challenged it. Here's what we learned.

The Good News

✓ The market is huge

Youth sports travel is a $19.2 billion market (pre-COVID), projected to grow to $52+ billion. 35+ million kids participate in organized youth sports. Families spend $1,200-1,500 per tournament weekend.

✓ The math works across sports

We validated clustered scheduling for soccer, basketball, baseball, softball, lacrosse, volleyball, hockey, and flag football. With proper time limits and rest periods, all major sports can support the format.

See: Sport-by-Sport Analysis

✓ Families do complain about wasted time

Tournament weekends are exhausting. Families report feeling like they're "hostage to the schedule" and that the experience is more stressful than enjoyable.

The Uncomfortable Discoveries

Wave scheduling already exists

Some tournaments have offered morning/afternoon sessions for 30+ years. Example: The Poughkeepsie Columbus Day Tournament (running since 1988) offers a morning session (8am-12:30pm) and afternoon session (1pm-5:30pm). This isn't new.

✗ Scheduling software already handles this

Platforms like Fastbreak AI already group games by division, handle rest periods, and optimize field usage. "Cluster games per team" is a solvable scheduling problem—it's not an innovation.

✗ Parent complaints aren't about scattered schedules

When we looked at what parents actually complain about, it's: burnout from too many tournaments, travel costs, politics and playing time, and coach behavior. "Scattered schedule" doesn't rank highly.

? TDs don't currently use wave scheduling—but why?

If wave scheduling is easy and beneficial, why don't more TDs do it? We don't actually know. Possible reasons: wave preference creates conflict (everyone wants morning), no financial incentive, or simply inertia.

What This Means

Our thesis had two parts:

Part 1: Clustered scheduling is novel and valuable.

→ Finding: It's not novel. It exists. Software can do it.

Part 2: Monetizing the free time through an activity marketplace.

→ Finding: We haven't validated this. We don't know if families would book activities, or if TDs would participate in revenue share.

"If wave scheduling is so easy, what's actually the opportunity? Is the scheduling the innovation—or is it something else?"
