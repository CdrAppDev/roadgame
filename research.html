What We Found

We did deep research across academic sources, industry publications, parent forums, tournament operators, and adjacent markets. Here's what we learned.

Each finding is backed by sources. View our research sources and confidence levels →

Phase 1: The Problem is Real

✓ The market is huge

Youth sports travel is a $52 billion market (2024). 35+ million kids participate in organized youth sports. Families spend $1,200-1,500 per tournament weekend. Private equity is consolidating aggressively.

✓ The "hostage" experience is real

Families spend 60-70% of tournament day NOT watching games. A typical 10-12 hour day includes only 2-3 hours of actual game time. The rest is waiting, driving between fields, and "liminal gaps" of dead time.

✓ Scheduling chaos is the #1 parent complaint

Across all forums and research, unpredictable scheduling ranked as parents' most consistent frustration. Yet they've normalized it: "Over time, it's become less of a nuisance because that's just how these tournaments are run."

✓ Families want GROUP experiences

59% of families try to combine tournaments with activities. But they don't want solo tourism—they want team experiences. The team IS the social unit.

Phase 1: What Didn't Work

Wave scheduling isn't new

The Poughkeepsie Columbus Day Tournament has offered morning/afternoon sessions since 1988. Volleyball uses wave scheduling universally. This isn't an innovation we need to invent.

✗ TDs won't adopt wave scheduling

Why: Large clubs send 15 teams with only 6 coaches. TDs stagger games so one coach can cover multiple teams sequentially. Wave scheduling would create overlaps coaches can't handle. TDs prioritize big clubs (their customers) over family convenience.

Key insight: "Staggered schedules aren't bad scheduling—they're intentional accommodation of under-staffed clubs."

Phase 2: The Disruption Research

We then asked: what if we don't try to change existing tournaments—what if we CREATE family-first tournaments that compete with the status quo?

Major Finding: Family-first tournaments already work at scale

This isn't theoretical. Destination tournaments have proven the model works—and commands premium pricing.

Operator Model Proof Point
Cooperstown Week-long, destination Sold 80% stake for $116 million
Ripken Experience Week-long, NO stay-to-play 350,000+ guests/year, expanding
Surf Cup 1 game/day, elite soccer $120M annual economic impact
Youth Baseball Nationals Guaranteed day off + theme parks Free theme park passes for players

✓ Families pay 2-3x premiums for predictable experiences

Cooperstown charges $10,000/team (vs $400-700 standard). Ripken charges $3,000+/week. Families pay because the experience justifies it.

✓ Wave scheduling works with no reversions

Volleyball has universally adopted wave scheduling (AM/PM blocks). Teams know exact windows weeks in advance. No documented cases of tournaments trying wave scheduling and reverting.

✓ Early adopter clubs exist

Three segments have dedicated coaching and would prefer wave scheduling:

  • Premium clubs (ECNL, MLS NEXT): Mandate dedicated coaches per team
  • Volleyball (all levels): Built-in dual-coach model
  • Small volunteer clubs: Natural 1:1 parent-coach ratio

✓ "No stay-to-play" is a competitive differentiator

Ripken explicitly markets: "This is your family vacation, so you choose to stay where you're most comfortable—no restrictions." Families resent mandatory hotel bookings. Abandoning stay-to-play builds trust.

✓ Adjacent markets prove the model

Dance and cheer competitions now compete on scheduling reliability. "Runs on time" is a loyalty driver. i9 Sports built a $100M+ business entirely on family-first convenience.

The Economics Work

✓ Family-first requires different economics—but they're proven

Traditional tournaments maximize field utilization. Family-first tournaments maximize per-family revenue:

Traditional Model Family-First Model
$400-700/team $1,500-3,000/team
Weekend (2-3 nights) Week-long (5-6 nights)
8-10 games/field/day 4-5 games/field/day
Extract hotel rebates Premium registration
Families as captive audience Families as vacation customers

Key Insight: The opportunity is to apply a proven model, not invent something new

Family-first destination tournaments work at scale. The question isn't whether the model works—it's whether we can execute it in underserved markets and sports.

What This Means

"The opportunity exists precisely because traditional operators optimize for the wrong metrics. Field utilization and hotel rebates matter to operators—but families increasingly resent experiences that feel extractive rather than memorable."

The research validates a disruption opportunity:

  • Demand exists: Families pay 2-3x premiums for predictable, vacation-integrated experiences
  • Supply exists: Premium clubs, volleyball, and small clubs have dedicated coaching
  • Model is proven: Ripken, Cooperstown, Surf Cup demonstrate success at scale
  • No failures documented: No evidence of wave scheduling being tried and abandoned
