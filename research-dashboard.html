What Is This Market?

Youth sports has transformed from kids playing at local parks into a massive travel industry. Millions of families now spend their weekends driving to tournaments in other cities, booking hotels, eating at restaurants, and filling time between games. This is the "youth sports travel market" — all the money families spend when they travel for their kids' tournaments.

$52.2B
What Families Spend Each Year on Tournament Travel
This is the total amount American families spent in 2023 on hotels, gas, food, and tournament fees when traveling for youth sports. To put it in perspective, that's more than the entire movie theater industry.
Growing 32% since 2021
$128B
Total Money This Pumps Into the Economy
Beyond what families spend directly, this money ripples through local economies — hotels hire more staff, restaurants buy more food, gas stations sell more fuel. This "ripple effect" more than doubles the economic impact.
Growing 39% since 2021
204.9M
Number of Sports Trips Taken Per Year
Americans took nearly 205 million trips for youth and amateur sports in 2023. That's more than one trip for every adult in the country. Most of these are weekend tournament trips within a few hours' drive.
Up 7% from last year
73.5M
Hotel Rooms Booked Per Year
Youth sports fills 73.5 million hotel room nights each year. Hotels in cities near sports complexes depend heavily on this business — it's often their most reliable revenue source, more predictable than business travel or vacations.
Up 10% since 2021
$1,016
What the Average Family Spends Per Year on Their Kid's Main Sport
This covers registration fees, equipment, training, and travel. But this is just the average — families in competitive travel leagues often spend $3,000 to $10,000+ per year, especially in expensive sports like hockey or competitive gymnastics.
Up 46% in just 5 years — costs are rising fast
$113.6B
Where This Market Is Headed by 2033
Industry analysts project the market will more than double over the next decade. Youth sports is becoming more professionalized, with more travel, more tournaments, and more spending. This is a growth market.
9.8% annual growth expected

What Does a Typical Tournament Trip Look Like?

Understanding the rhythm of a tournament weekend helps explain where the frustrations come from. These aren't relaxing vacations — they're logistically intense, exhausting experiences for the whole family.

Where the Money Goes (Per Trip)

Each tournament weekend costs families around $800-$1,000 when you add up hotels, gas, food, and fees. Here's how that typically breaks down. Notice that lodging and food are the biggest chunks — these are the areas where families feel the most pain.

Which Sports Drive the Most Travel?

Basketball is the most popular youth sport, but baseball and soccer families often travel the most miles. "Travel ball" culture is especially intense in baseball, where teams may play 40-60 games per season, most of them requiring overnight trips.

Where Do These Families Live?

Travel sports families are concentrated in suburban and urban areas. Rural families participate less because they have fewer travel team options nearby and longer drives to reach tournament venues. This tells us where our early users likely are.

How Fast Is This Market Growing?

The dip in 2021 was COVID. Notice how fast it bounced back and then exceeded pre-pandemic levels. Youth sports travel is seen as "non-negotiable" by families — even in tough economic times, parents prioritize their kids' sports.

The Typical Tournament Weekend

Friday: Leave work early, drive 2-4 hours, check into hotel, find dinner for the family
Saturday: Wake up at 6am, games at 8am, noon, and 4pm with long gaps in between. Fill 8-10 hours at the venue or nearby. Team dinner somewhere that can seat 25 people.
Sunday: Bracket play, could end at 10am (if you lose) or 5pm (if you win). Drive home exhausted.

This happens 10-15 weekends per year for serious travel teams. That's a lot of hotels, a lot of meals, and a lot of "what do we do for the next 3 hours?"

What Makes Families Frustrated?

Through research into parent forums, Reddit discussions, and industry reports, we identified the biggest sources of frustration. These are ranked by how intensely parents complain about them — the higher the bar, the more anger we found.

#1: "Stay to Play" Hotel Mandates — The Biggest Source of Anger

What is this? Many tournaments require teams to book hotels through an "official" booking portal to be eligible to play. You can't just book on Expedia or use your hotel points.
Families pay $80-$100 MORE per night than public rates
The "discounted" portal rate is often higher than what you'd find on Booking.com or the hotel's own website. Parents can't use their loyalty points or status. Cancellation policies are rigid — if your kid gets sick, you still pay. Hidden kickbacks of $10-20 per room night go back to tournament organizers.
Why this matters for RoadGame: This is a captive audience with no good options. If we can add value to this mandatory experience (better info, group coordination, local recommendations), families will love us for making a frustrating requirement actually useful.

#2: Chaotic Schedules and Exhausting Days

What is this? Tournament schedules are often poorly designed, leaving families stuck at venues for 8-10 hours with long gaps between games and little rest time.
Families spend 8-10 hours per day at the venue
Games might be at 8am, then 1pm, then 6pm — leaving huge "dead time" gaps where you're stuck near the fields with nothing to do. Game times change at the last minute. Kids are sleep-deprived (8pm game Friday, 8am game Saturday). Parents often learn about schedule changes "through the kid."
Why this matters for RoadGame: These "gaps" are exactly the time families need help. "We have 3 hours — where can we eat? What can the siblings do? Is there anywhere with air conditioning?" This is the problem we're solving.

#3: Too Many Apps, None of Them Talk to Each Other

What is this? Families juggle 4-5 different apps for different purposes — one for game schedules, one for team chat, one for stats, one for registration — and none of them integrate.
4-5 apps per family, with different apps for each sport/team
GameChanger for scores, TeamSnap for team communication, GroupMe for quick messages, SportsEngine for registration, plus the tournament's own website. If you have two kids in different sports, double everything. Parents describe "app fatigue" and wish there was one place for everything.
Why this matters for RoadGame: There's no single "tournament weekend companion" that handles the travel experience. We're not competing with TeamSnap (they do communication) — we're filling a gap no one addresses.

#4: Finding Restaurants That Can Handle 25 People

What is this? Teams want to eat together, but finding a restaurant that can seat a group of 20-30 people (kids plus parents) on short notice is nearly impossible.
Need to seat 20-30 people with only 90 minutes between games
Most restaurants can't handle a group this size without a reservation. Splitting the check is a nightmare. You need somewhere fast (limited time), kid-friendly (picky eaters), and affordable (families are already spending so much). Yelp doesn't help because it doesn't tell you "can this place handle a baseball team?"
Why this matters for RoadGame: "Where should the team eat?" is asked dozens of times per weekend. A curated list of "tournament-ready" restaurants near the venue — with info about group capacity and speed of service — would be incredibly valuable.

#5: Burnout — Kids and Parents Are Exhausted

What is this? The intensity of travel sports — every weekend, all season — leads to physical and emotional exhaustion. Many kids quit, and parents question if it's worth it.
70% of kids quit organized sports by age 13
Parents spend 20+ hours per week on sports logistics. Siblings get dragged along and neglected. Family time disappears. The sport becomes a "chore" instead of fun. Parents describe feeling trapped — they've invested so much money and time, but they're not enjoying it.
Why this matters for RoadGame: We can't solve burnout entirely, but we can reduce the logistical burden. If the tournament weekend is less stressful to plan and navigate, families might actually enjoy parts of it.

Who Else Is Trying to Solve This Problem?

Several companies operate in this space, but each focuses on a specific slice of the experience. No one owns the full "tournament weekend" — especially the family experience between games.

Lucid Travel

Lucid helps teams book hotels with automated tools. They return 5% of the hotel commission as cashback to the club. They've grown fast by making hotel booking less painful for team coordinators.
What they don't do: They only handle hotels. No restaurant recommendations, no activity suggestions, no help with "what do we do for the next 3 hours?" They're a booking tool, not a trip companion.

Coordle

The closest thing to what we're building. Coordle offers itinerary management, local restaurant ads, and group meal ordering. They're specifically targeting the "chaos" of tournament weekends.
What they don't do: They sell to organizations (B2B), not directly to families. You can only use it if your club has adopted it. Limited market footprint so far. Invite-only access.

TeamSnap

The dominant app for youth sports team management. 24+ million users. Handles schedules, team chat, availability tracking, and registration. Almost every travel team uses it.
What they don't do: No travel features. No hotel booking, no local recommendations, no tournament-specific tools. They've partnered with restaurant chains for advertising, but there's no actual discovery or booking functionality.

Team Travel Source / HBC Event Services

These are the traditional "housing agencies" that work with tournaments to manage hotel blocks. They're the ones running the "Stay to Play" portals that families complain about.
What they don't do: Their customer is the tournament director, not the family. They're seen as the "enforcers" of unpopular policies. No incentive to make the family experience better — they just need rooms booked.
The Big Gap No One Fills
"Bracket-to-Table" Connection: No platform connects the live tournament schedule (when are your games, when are your gaps) to real-time recommendations for what to do during those gaps. This is the white space RoadGame can own.

How Do You Start a Marketplace From Zero?

RoadGame is a "two-sided marketplace" — we need families (demand) AND local businesses like restaurants and hotels (supply). The classic problem: families won't come if there are no listings, but businesses won't list if there are no families. Here's how successful companies solved this "chicken and egg" problem.

1. Build Something Useful for One Side First

Don't try to attract both sides at once. Build a tool that's valuable to one side even if the other side doesn't exist yet. This gives you a "foot in the door."
How OpenTable did it: They built reservation management software that restaurants could use internally. Restaurants signed up for the software benefit — then OpenTable turned on the consumer booking site once they had enough restaurants.
For RoadGame: We can provide value to families by showing scraped listings (from Google) and helping with group coordination — even before any businesses have "signed up."

2. Go Where Your Users Already Are

Don't try to build a new audience from scratch. Find where your target users are already hanging out and meet them there. Redirect their attention to your platform.
How Airbnb did it: They built a tool that let hosts cross-post their listings to Craigslist automatically. This "parasitized" Craigslist's existing audience and funneled them to Airbnb.
For RoadGame: Target Facebook groups where sports parents discuss travel, team coordinator group chats, and tournament websites. Meet families where they already are.

3. Focus on One Event to Start

Instead of launching everywhere at once, pick ONE event or location and achieve 100% density there. It's better to be complete in one place than thin everywhere.
How Airbnb did it: They launched at the Democratic National Convention in Denver — a temporary spike in demand that they could fully serve. It proved the model worked before expanding.
For RoadGame: Pick ONE tournament from an existing relationship. Serve it completely. Have every nearby restaurant and hotel listed. Then use that success story to expand.

4. Do Things That Don't Scale (At First)

In the early days, do things manually that you'll eventually automate. This builds quality and trust that wouldn't be possible otherwise.
How Airbnb did it: The founders personally traveled to New York and took professional photos of listings. This made listings look trustworthy and increased bookings 2.5x.
For RoadGame: Manually curate the first set of listings. Call hotels to check on team amenities. Visit restaurants to verify they can handle large groups. Build quality that can't be scraped.

5. Create Listings Without Asking (Then Let Them Claim)

Don't wait for businesses to sign up. Scrape public data to create listings, drive traffic to those listings, then reach out and say "you already have 50 views, want to claim this?"
How Yelp did it: They scraped business listings from the Yellow Pages so users could find any business. Businesses then "claimed" their listing to update it, respond to reviews, etc.
For RoadGame: Use Google Places to create initial listings near tournament venues. Track views and interest. Then reach out to businesses with proof: "Tournament families looked at your listing 47 times last weekend. Want to claim it?"

6. Build Authority Through Local Partnerships

Partner with local businesses or media to build credibility and drive traffic. Offer something valuable in exchange for visibility.
How GrubHub did it: They offered $10 discounts to local newspaper readers. This earned them backlinks (good for SEO) and introduced their service to local audiences.
For RoadGame: Partner with local restaurants to offer "Team Meal Deals" (10% off for groups in jerseys). Get listed on the tournament's official website. Build local authority.

The RoadGame Opportunity — Summarized

Youth sports travel is a $52 billion market where families are systematically frustrated. They're forced into overpriced hotels, stuck at venues for 10 hours with nothing to do, and juggling 5 apps just to manage a weekend.

Current solutions focus on the tournament organizer (housing mandates, registration) or the athlete (scores, brackets, stats). No one is solving for the family.

RoadGame will own the "in-between moments": the 3-hour gap between games, the team dinner coordination, the sibling entertainment, the "where do we eat?" question asked 10 times per weekend.

We'll start by scraping provider data, serving one tournament completely, and proving demand. Then we'll show businesses: "You already have traffic. Want to claim your listing?" This is how Yelp, Airbnb, and OpenTable all got started.
